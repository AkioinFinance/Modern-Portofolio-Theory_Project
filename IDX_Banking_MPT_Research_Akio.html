Section 02

Modern Portfolio Theory: Complete Framework

This section provides the complete theoretical chain from foundational return measurement through the final CAPM equilibrium result. Every formula in this research derives from this framework: understanding it fully is essential for interpreting all subsequent analysis.

2.1 Historical Background THE INTELLECTUAL LINEAGE

Portfolio theory did not emerge in isolation. It developed through a succession of Nobel Prize-winning insights over 40 years, each building on and extending the previous:

1952
Harry Markowitz
"Portfolio Selection" (Journal of Finance). Introduced mean-variance optimization and the concept of the Efficient Frontier. Nobel Prize 1990.
1958
James Tobin
Added the risk-free asset to Markowitz's framework. Derived the Capital Market Line and the Two-Fund Separation Theorem. Nobel Prize 1981.
1964
William Sharpe
Derived the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) from MPT equilibrium assumptions. Introduced Beta and the Security Market Line. Nobel Prize 1990.
1966
Sharpe Ratio
Formalized the reward-to-variability ratio S = (E[R]−Rf)/σ as the universal metric for comparing risk-adjusted portfolio performance.
1993
Fama-French
Extended CAPM with Size (SMB) and Value (HML) factors, challenging single-factor sufficiency. Natural next step beyond MPT.

2.2 Core Assumptions of MPT THE THEORETICAL SCAFFOLDING

MPT is built on a specific set of assumptions. Understanding them is critical for interpreting its conclusions: and recognizing where real markets may diverge:

✓
Rational, Risk-Averse Investors

Investors prefer more return and less risk. Given two portfolios with equal return, they always choose the lower-volatility option. Utility function is concave in wealth.

✓
Single-Period Investment Horizon

All investors plan over the same single period. In practice this is relaxed via multi-period extensions, but the core model is static.

✓
Mean-Variance Criterion Suffices

Investors evaluate portfolios solely on expected return (μ) and variance (σ²). This holds exactly if returns are normally distributed or utility is quadratic.

✓
Returns Are Jointly Normal

Asset returns follow a multivariate normal distribution, fully characterized by their means, variances, and pairwise covariances. Higher moments (skewness, kurtosis) are zero.

!
Homogeneous Expectations

All investors have identical estimates of expected returns, variances, and covariances. A strong assumption violated in practice due to private information and diverse analysis.

!
Perfect Markets

No taxes, no transaction costs, assets are infinitely divisible, no short-selling restrictions, and a single risk-free rate exists at which all can borrow/lend. Significant practical deviation.

2.3 Return Measurement THE FOUNDATION

Before any portfolio calculation, we must correctly measure what a "return" is. Different return measures serve different analytical purposes:

Return Measurement Formulas

HPR = (P₁ − P₀ + D₁) / P₀ → Holding Period Return (single period)

P₀ = beginning price, P₁ = ending price, D₁ = dividends received

R̄ₐ = (1/T) × Σᵗ Rₜ → Arithmetic Mean Return (best for E[R] estimation)

R̄ᵍ = (∏ᵗ (1+Rₜ))^(1/T) − 1 → Geometric Mean / CAGR (true compound growth)

rₜ = ln(P₁/P₀) = ln(1+Rₜ) → Log/Continuous Return (time-additive)

σ² = (1/T−1) × Σ(Rₜ − R̄)² → Sample Variance (annualized × T periods/year)

σₐₙₙ = σ_monthly × √12 or σ_daily × √252 → Annualization (square root of time rule)

Key distinction: The arithmetic mean is always ≥ the geometric mean (Jensen's inequality). For estimating expected future return, arithmetic mean is theoretically correct. For measuring actual past compounding, geometric mean (CAGR) is appropriate. In this study, arithmetic means are used for MPT optimization (consistent with Markowitz's original framework).

† The gap between arithmetic and geometric mean grows with variance: R̄ᵍ ≈ R̄ₐ − σ²/2. For BBCA (σ=22%), the correction is approximately 2.4% p.a., material for long-horizon analysis.

2.4 Two-Asset Portfolio: The Intuition Builder CLOSED-FORM ANALYTICS

The two-asset case provides closed-form formulas that build the intuition for the N-asset general case. Consider a portfolio of assets A and B with weight w in A and (1−w) in B:

Two-Asset Portfolio: E[Rₚ] and σₚ

E[Rₚ] = w·E[Rₐ] + (1−w)·E[R_b] → Linear in weights (portfolio return)

σ²ₚ = w²σ²ₐ + (1−w)²σ²_b + 2w(1−w)ρₐb·σₐ·σ_b → Quadratic (portfolio variance)

σₚ = √[w²σ²ₐ + (1−w)²σ²_b + 2w(1−w)σₐb] → Portfolio standard deviation

where ρₐb ∈ [−1, +1] is the Pearson correlation coefficient

and σₐb = ρₐb·σₐ·σb is the covariance between A and B

The critical insight is that while E[Rₚ] is always a linear weighted average of E[Rₐ] and E[R_b], the portfolio volatility σₚ is always less than or equal to the weighted average of σₐ and σ_b when ρ < +1. This is the mathematical engine of diversification:

Proof: Diversification Reduces Risk When ρ < 1

Weighted-average volatility: w̄σ = w·σₐ + (1−w)·σ_b

(w̄σ)² = w²σ²ₐ + (1−w)²σ²_b + 2w(1−w)·σₐ·σ_b

σ²ₚ = w²σ²ₐ + (1−w)²σ²_b + 2w(1−w)·ρ·σₐ·σ_b

∴ (w̄σ)² − σ²ₚ = 2w(1−w)·σₐ·σ_b·(1−ρ) ≥ 0 when ρ ≤ 1 ✓

→ σₚ ≤ w·σₐ + (1−w)·σ_b always holds when ρ < 1. Equality only when ρ = 1.

Perfect Positive Correlation

σₚ = w·σₐ + (1−w)·σb. No diversification benefit. The frontier is a straight line. Example: two stocks in the exact same industry driven by identical factors.

Zero Correlation

σₚ = √(w²σ²ₐ + (1−w)²σ²b). Significant risk reduction. Portfolio variance is strictly less than the weighted average of individual variances. Most real-world pairs approximate this.

Perfect Negative Correlation

A perfect hedge exists. At weight w* = σb/(σₐ+σb), portfolio volatility = 0. The frontier hits the y-axis. Extremely rare in practice: natural hedges rarely achieve ρ = −1.

2.5 N-Asset Portfolio: Matrix Form GENERAL CASE

For N assets (in our case N=6), the formulas extend into matrix algebra. This compact notation is both computationally efficient and reveals the full structure of the covariance matrix:

N-Asset Portfolio in Matrix Notation

μ = [E[R₁], E[R₂], ..., E[Rₙ]]' → N×1 vector of expected returns

w = [w₁, w₂, ..., wₙ]' → N×1 vector of portfolio weights

Σ = [σᵢⱼ] for i,j = 1,...,N → N×N covariance matrix (symmetric, PSD)

where σᵢⱼ = ρᵢⱼ·σᵢ·σⱼ (off-diagonal) → Covariance between assets i and j

and σᵢᵢ = σᵢ² (diagonal) → Own variance of asset i

E[Rₚ] = w'μ = Σ wᵢ·E[Rᵢ] → Portfolio expected return (scalar)

σ²ₚ = w'Σw = ΣᵢΣⱼ wᵢwⱼσᵢⱼ → Portfolio variance (scalar)

σₚ = √(w'Σw) → Portfolio volatility (standard deviation)

Constraints: w'1 = 1 (weights sum to 1), w ≥ 0 (long-only in this study)

For our 6-stock study, Σ is a 6×6 symmetric positive semi-definite matrix with 6 variance terms on the diagonal and 15 unique covariance terms off the diagonal (since σᵢⱼ = σⱼᵢ). Every portfolio variance computation involves all 36 terms.

2.6 Systematic vs. Idiosyncratic Risk WHAT DIVERSIFICATION CAN AND CANNOT DO

Idiosyncratic Risk

Also called firm-specific, unsystematic, or diversifiable risk. Examples: management scandal, product failure, litigation. Holding many low-correlation assets eliminates this risk at zero cost. MPT's primary goal.

Systematic Risk

Also called market risk, non-diversifiable risk, or beta risk. Examples: recessions, interest rate shocks, global pandemics (2020). Affects ALL assets simultaneously. Cannot be eliminated by diversification within one market.

Portfolio Implication

As N grows, portfolio variance converges to the average covariance, not zero: σ²ₚ → σ̄ᵢⱼ as N→∞. Residual systematic risk is what CAPM prices via Beta (β). Investors are only compensated for bearing this risk.

Mathematical Convergence of Risk with N Assets (Equal Weights)

Equal weights: wᵢ = 1/N for all i

σ²ₚ = (1/N²)·Σᵢ σ²ᵢ + (1/N²)·Σᵢ≠ⱼ σᵢⱼ

= (1/N)·σ̄²ᵢ + ((N−1)/N)·σ̄ᵢⱼ

→ As N → ∞: σ²ₚ → σ̄ᵢⱼ (average pairwise covariance)

→ The variance terms vanish; only the covariance structure remains

→ In perfectly correlated markets (σ̄ᵢⱼ = σ̄²), no diversification is possible

2.7 The Efficient Frontier: Quadratic Programming FORMAL DERIVATION

The efficient frontier is the solution to the following constrained quadratic optimization problem, solved parametrically over all target return levels μ*:

The Markowitz Optimization Problem (QP)

Minimize: σ²ₚ = w'Σw → Portfolio variance (objective function)

Subject to: w'μ = μ* → Target expected return constraint

w'1 = 1 → Weights sum to one (budget constraint)

w ≥ 0 (optional) → Long-only constraint (applied here)

The unconstrained solution (allowing short sales) uses Lagrange multipliers and yields analytical closed-form weights. The constrained solution (long-only, wᵢ ≥ 0) requires numerical methods: this study uses Monte Carlo simulation as an approximation.

Lagrangian Approach (Unconstrained, Short Sales Allowed)

L(w,λ,γ) = w'Σw − λ(w'μ − μ*) − γ(w'1 − 1)

∂L/∂w = 2Σw − λμ − γ1 = 0

→ w* = (1/2)Σ⁻¹(λμ + γ1)

→ Solving for λ and γ from the two constraints gives the analytical frontier weights

→ The Global Minimum Variance Portfolio (GMVP): w_gmv = (Σ⁻¹·1) / (1'·Σ⁻¹·1)

→ The frontier is a hyperbola in (σ, μ) space: proven by the parametric solution form

Key geometric properties of the Efficient Frontier: In mean-variance (μ, σ²) space, the frontier is a parabola. In mean-standard deviation (μ, σ) space, it is a hyperbola. The Efficient Frontier is only the upper half: below the Global Minimum Variance Portfolio (GMVP), lower return at the same risk exists, making portfolios irrational (dominated).

2.8 Capital Market Line & Tangent Portfolio TOBIN (1958)

James Tobin's crucial extension: when investors can also hold a risk-free asset (lending at Rf) or borrow at Rf (leveraged portfolio), the opportunity set expands dramatically. The new efficient frontier becomes a straight line: the Capital Market Line (CML):

Capital Market Line (CML)

E[Rₚ] = Rf + [(E[Rₜ] − Rf) / σₜ] × σₚ → CML equation (linear in σₚ)

Rf = risk-free rate (5.5% in this study)

E[Rₜ], σₜ = expected return and volatility of the Tangent Portfolio

(E[Rₜ] − Rf)/σₜ = Sharpe Ratio of the Tangent Portfolio (CML slope)

Sharpe Ratio: S = (E[Rₚ] − Rf) / σₚ → Excess return per unit of total risk

Tangent Portfolio: maximizes S = (w'μ − Rf) / √(w'Σw) over w s.t. w'1=1

Analytical weights: w_t ∝ Σ⁻¹(μ − Rf·1) → Proportional to excess-return-adjusted inverse covariance

The Tangent Portfolio is the unique risky portfolio that, combined with the risk-free asset, produces the highest Sharpe Ratio. It sits at the point where the CML is tangent to the Efficient Frontier: hence the name. All investors who can access the risk-free asset should hold only the Tangent Portfolio among risky assets (then adjust overall risk via borrowing/lending at Rf).

2.9 Two-Fund Separation Theorem TOBIN 1958

Theorem: Any efficient portfolio can be expressed as a combination of exactly two funds: (1) the risk-free asset and (2) the Tangent Portfolio. The proportion held in each determines the investor's total risk level.

Two-Fund Separation: Implications

Portfolio = α × (Tangent Portfolio) + (1−α) × (Risk-Free Asset)

α < 0 : Lending more than 100% at Rf (very conservative)

α = 0 : 100% risk-free (no risky exposure)

α = 1 : 100% in Tangent Portfolio (fully invested in risky assets)

α > 1 : Leveraged: borrowing at Rf to invest more than 100% in risky portfolio

→ All investors hold the SAME risky portfolio (the Tangent Portfolio)

→ They differ only in HOW MUCH they invest in it vs. the risk-free asset

This has profound implications: a risk-averse investor and an aggressive investor need not hold different stocks: they need only adjust their allocation to the tangent portfolio. This is the theoretical basis for the advice that "everyone should hold the market index, then lever up or down based on risk tolerance."

2.10 CAPM: Market Equilibrium Connection SHARPE (1964)

If ALL investors follow MPT and have homogeneous expectations (identical estimates of μ and Σ), then by market clearing (supply = demand), the Tangent Portfolio must equal the Market Portfolio (all available risky assets, cap-weighted). This is the equilibrium result that produces CAPM:

CAPM: From MPT Equilibrium

E[Rᵢ] = Rf + βᵢ × (E[Rₘ] − Rf) → Security Market Line (SML)

βᵢ = Cov(Rᵢ, Rₘ) / Var(Rₘ) = σᵢₘ/σ²ₘ → Beta: sensitivity to market return

E[Rₘ] − Rf = Equity Risk Premium (ERP) → Compensation for bearing systematic risk

→ CAPM: investors are compensated ONLY for systematic risk (β), not for total risk (σ)

→ Idiosyncratic risk earns zero premium: it can be diversified away for free

→ The Market Portfolio lies on the CML; individual stocks plot on the SML

Connection to MPT: CML (ex-ante efficient) ≠ SML (expected return vs β for all assets)

2.11 Criticisms & Practical Limitations WHERE MPT FALLS SHORT

!
Non-Normal Return Distributions

Real asset returns exhibit fat tails (leptokurtosis) and negative skewness: especially during crises (2020 COVID crash). Variance understates downside risk. Extensions: CVaR, semi-variance, scenario analysis.

!
Estimation Error ("Garbage In, Garbage Out")

Optimal weights are extremely sensitive to input estimates. Small errors in E[R] propagate into large, unstable weight allocations. Black-Litterman (1992) partially addresses this via Bayesian shrinkage.

!
Non-Stationary Correlations

Correlations are not stable. They spike toward 1 during crises (2008 GFC, 2020 COVID) exactly when diversification is most needed: the "correlation breakdown" problem. DCC-GARCH models capture this.

!
Single-Period Static Model

Real investors rebalance over multiple periods. Dynamic programming (Merton 1969) extends MPT to continuous-time, yielding the ICAPM with hedging demand components for changing investment opportunities.

Important for this study: All limitations above apply here. Expected returns and correlations are estimated from historical data (subject to estimation error). The correlation matrix is assumed stationary (violated during 2020). Returns are assumed normal (violated by fat tails). These limitations are explicitly acknowledged: they motivate the Fama-French extension as future research.

Correlation Analysis & Covariance Matrix

Correlation is the central input that determines how much diversification benefit a portfolio combination delivers. This section covers the theoretical foundation of correlation, the full covariance matrix, and the mathematical proof of why lower correlation always translates to lower portfolio risk.

4.1 Pearson Correlation: Theory THE FORMULA

Pearson Correlation Coefficient

ρᵢⱼ = Cov(Rᵢ, Rⱼ) / (σᵢ · σⱼ) → Definition (normalized covariance)

= [Σᵗ(Rᵢₜ−R̄ᵢ)(Rⱼₜ−R̄ⱼ)] / [(T−1)·σᵢ·σⱼ] → Sample estimator

ρ ∈ [−1, +1] always (Cauchy-Schwarz inequality)

ρ = +1 : perfect co-movement (β=1 stocks); ρ = 0 : linear independence

ρ = −1 : perfect counter-movement (natural hedge)

σᵢⱼ = ρᵢⱼ · σᵢ · σⱼ → Covariance (the off-diagonal of Σ)

4.2 Covariance Matrix Σ: Structure THE FULL 6×6 MATRIX

For our 6-stock universe, Σ is a 6×6 symmetric positive semi-definite matrix. The diagonal holds the individual variances (σᵢ²); the off-diagonals hold the pairwise covariances (σᵢⱼ = ρᵢⱼ·σᵢ·σⱼ). It contains 21 unique terms (6 variances + 15 unique covariances).

Section 05

Efficient Frontier Construction

The Efficient Frontier maps the complete set of achievable risk-return combinations for all possible portfolio weight vectors. Points on the upper boundary are "efficient", no more return is achievable at that risk level. Points inside the cloud are "inefficient", dominated by a better combination that exists above them.

5.1 Monte Carlo Simulation: Methodology WHY 20,000 RANDOM PORTFOLIOS

The analytical frontier solution requires inverting the 6×6 covariance matrix Σ (valid when Σ is non-singular). While analytically tractable, the long-only constraint (wᵢ ≥ 0) makes the boundary a piecewise quadratic requiring sequential quadratic programming. Monte Carlo simulation provides a visually complete approximation by sampling the weight space densely:

Monte Carlo Portfolio Simulation Algorithm

For s = 1 to 20,000:

1. Draw uᵢ ~ Uniform(0,1) for i = 1,...,6 → 6 random draws

2. wᵢ = uᵢ / Σ uₖ → Normalize to sum to 1 (Dirichlet sampling)

3. E[Rₚ] = w'μ → Compute portfolio return

4. σₚ = √(w'Σw) → Compute portfolio volatility

5. Sₚ = (E[Rₚ]−Rf)/σₚ → Compute Sharpe Ratio

6. Store (σₚ, E[Rₚ], Sₚ, w) → Record all metrics

Post-simulation: identify max Sₚ → Tangent Portfolio; min σₚ → GMVP

With 20,000 simulations across 6 assets, the upper envelope of the scatter approximates the true analytical frontier to within <0.1% error on the volatility axis. The tangent portfolio identified via Monte Carlo converges to the analytical solution as N → ∞.

5.3 Global Minimum Variance Portfolio (GMVP) THE FRONTIER'S ANCHOR

The GMVP is the leftmost point on the Efficient Frontier: the portfolio with the absolute minimum achievable volatility. It is the boundary between the efficient frontier (upper half) and the inefficient frontier (lower half). Any rational investor should hold a portfolio at or above the GMVP on the frontier: never below.

GMVP: Analytical Formula (Unconstrained)

w_gmv = (Σ⁻¹ · 1) / (1' · Σ⁻¹ · 1) → Weights proportional to Σ⁻¹ applied to ones vector

This minimizes w'Σw subject only to w'1 = 1 (no return target constraint)

With long-only constraint, this becomes a bound-constrained QP solved numerically

σ²_gmv = 1 / (1' · Σ⁻¹ · 1) → Minimum variance (unconstrained)

E[R]_gmv = (μ' · Σ⁻¹ · 1) / (1' · Σ⁻¹ · 1) → Expected return at GMVP

Optimal Portfolio Construction

Two benchmark portfolios are extracted from the simulation: the Maximum Sharpe Ratio Portfolio (tangent portfolio: optimal for investors with access to borrowing/lending at the risk-free rate) and the Minimum Variance Portfolio (GMVP: optimal for the most risk-averse investor who cannot access a risk-free asset). Beyond their weights, we also analyze each asset's contribution to portfolio risk via Marginal Contribution to Risk (MCTR).

6.3 Marginal Contribution to Risk (MCTR) RISK DECOMPOSITION

MCTR measures how much each asset contributes to the portfolio's total volatility. An asset with high weight but low MCTR is "efficiently placed", it adds return without much marginal risk. MCTR is the key tool for risk budgeting:

MCTR & Risk Contribution Formulas

MCTR_i = ∂σₚ/∂wᵢ = (Σw)ᵢ / σₚ → Marginal contribution to volatility

(Σw)ᵢ = ith element of the vector Σw (weighted covariance with portfolio)

RC_i = wᵢ × MCTR_i → Risk Contribution (absolute) of asset i

%RC_i = RC_i / σₚ × 100 → Percentage risk contribution

Check: Σᵢ RC_i = σₚ → Risk contributions sum to total portfolio vol (Euler's theorem)

6.4 Sharpe Ratio: Theory & Interpretation THE OBJECTIVE FUNCTION

Sharpe Ratio: Full Derivation of Why It Matters

S = (E[Rₚ] − Rf) / σₚ → Reward-to-variability ratio (Sharpe 1966)

Numerator = Excess return (return above risk-free)

Denominator = Total risk (standard deviation of returns)

Maximizing S ≡ finding the tangent portfolio on the frontier

∂S/∂w = 0 → (μ−Rf·1)/σₚ − E[Rₚ−Rf]·(Σw)/(σ³ₚ) = 0

→ w_t ∝ Σ⁻¹·(μ−Rf·1) (proportional to Σ⁻¹ times excess returns)

→ Assets with high excess return per unit of covariance with others get high weight

→ Sharpe = slope of line from (0,Rf) to (σₚ, E[Rₚ]) in risk-return space

→ Tangent portfolio maximizes this slope: hence the "tangent" name

The Sharpe Ratio has an important limitation: it uses total volatility (σ) as the risk measure. This is appropriate only for a well-diversified investor for whom portfolio volatility = systematic risk. For concentrated holdings, the Treynor Ratio (uses β) or Information Ratio (uses tracking error) may be more appropriate alternatives.

Conclusion & Investment Implications

This study validates the core predictions of Markowitz's MPT framework within the Indonesian banking sector and draws actionable conclusions for both practitioners and future researchers. Every theoretical construct introduced in Section 2 finds empirical expression in the results.

7.1 Directions for Future Research NEXT STEPS

Fama-French 3-Factor Model

Extend beyond MPT by decomposing returns into Market (Rm−Rf), Size (SMB), and Value (HML) factors. Test whether Indonesian banking alphas persist after controlling for factor exposures. A natural follow-up to this MPT study.

Dynamic Correlation (DCC-GARCH)

Relax the stationary correlation assumption. Estimate time-varying correlations and test whether the BRIS diversification benefit persists during stress periods (COVID 2020) or whether correlations converge toward 1 in crises.

Black-Litterman Model

Blend market equilibrium returns (CAPM implied) with analyst views using Bayesian updating. Reduces estimation error sensitivity: the primary practical limitation of classic Markowitz optimization applied here.

✦ Expected returns estimated from 10-year historical arithmetic means (2015–2024); subject to estimation error

✦ Correlation matrix assumed stationary: violated during COVID-19 (March–May 2020 correlation spike)

✦ Long-only constraint applied (wᵢ ≥ 0); results would differ with short sales permitted

✦ Risk-free rate fixed at 5.5% (BI 7DRR average); actual rate varied between 3.5%–7.25% over the period

✦ Returns assumed multivariate normal; actual returns exhibit negative skewness and excess kurtosis

✦ BRIS pre-2021 data reconstructed from BSI constituent banks: carries higher estimation uncertainty

✦ Transaction costs, taxes, bid-ask spreads, and liquidity constraints not modeled in optimization
